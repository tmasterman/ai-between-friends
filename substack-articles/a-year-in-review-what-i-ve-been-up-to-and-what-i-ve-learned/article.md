## Article

I recently sat down to update my personal website, the kind of activity it feels like you just worked on, but once you actually sit down, you quickly realize more time has passed than you thought. As I was looking at the site, two things stood out to me. First, it didn’t quite feel like me anymore and second, the last time I published anything about AI was almost a year ago to the day.

My first thought upon realizing this was “what have I been doing this year?”And my second thought was “actually, a lot!” Not all of it was what I expected or had planned, but most of it was really good for me and challenged me in ways I couldn’t have predicted.

So, I wanted to sit down and take a moment to reflect and share the main lessons I’ve been learning (and am frankly still working on) this year.

The more you know, the more you don’t (and that’s ok)

If I had to distill the last year professionally into two sentences it would be this: the more I learned, the more I realized I didn’t know. And at the same time, I also realized I was more capable than I’d given myself credit for.

Last May I left my role on the Applied AI Research team at Neudesic, an IBM company and joined an 8 person team at MeshAgent. When I decided it was time for a change professionally, one of the main things I was seeking was an environment where I would learn a ton and be challenged in new ways. I went from a tight knit team where we were exploring, applying, and creating new best practices and patterns for working with AI Agents, RAG, AI safety, and more to a product company focused on solving infrastructure and distribution challenges for agents. In my previous role, I spent a ton of time reading papers, testing patterns, conducting experiments, and sharing what we found with clients and AI communities. I loved the work, and the team, and still think it is some of the most rewarding work I’ve done in my career so far.

Stepping into a new role opened up an entirely new set of problems I hadn’t fully appreciated until I was on the team. It’s one thing to design a great agent. It’s another to understand what’s required to deploy that agent reliably, how to make it multiplayer, how to keep things secure, how to give the agent access to all the infrastructure and tooling it needs, and how to distribute what you’ve built across teams. The gap between applied research and building a product felt less like a step and more like a canyon. And while that canyon can be exhilarating, it can also be overwhelming.

I think this is part of why I didn’t write as much in 2025. When you learn something genuinely hard and new that’s changing so quickly, it can be challenging to find the right words to explain what you’re learning, why it matters, and feel confident in doing so.

Get comfortable with an unprecedented rate of change

AI advancements have only accelerated over the last year, we’ve seen huge jumps in model capability, the rise of coding agents and vibe coding, and increasingly sophisticated agent harnesses. While all of these advancements are incredibly exciting, it’s also impossible to keep up with all of them, and you will go crazy trying.

As AI adoption and capability continue to accelerate, I think it’s even more important to broaden your skillset and take a look at the bigger picture. For me, that meant going somewhere I could learn additional skills at the app and product layer, not just the AI layer. More roles are going to require interdisciplinary knowledge that previously would have been split across multiple distinct jobs.

My practical takeaway: find the things you are genuinely curious about and don’t give them up. The rest can wait. The internet is full of noise when it comes to AI, which makes it even more important to spend time personally exploring and building your own intuition on how to work with it effectively. There will always be more to learn, and you will probably surprise yourself with just how much you can accomplish.

Learn to be a communication chameleon

If there’s one skill I’ve been unexpectedly humbled by this year, it’s communication.

I always thought I was pretty good at articulating my thoughts and feedback, but naturally, there’s always room to improve. This year I’ve learned a lot about how to communicate and meet people where they are, in the way that they needregardless of my personal communication preferences. There is definitely something to be said for learning to be a “communication chameleon” and speaking to your audience the way they need to hear things even if it feels unnatural to you.

For me, I realized how comfortable I am with conflict and directness in my personal life, but howuncomfortable it still makes me feel professionally. This is definitely a skill I am still working on, but am proud of how far I’ve come. I think it’s easy to hesitate to be direct because you don’t want to come off as rude or insensitive, but the reality is you can be direct while still being respectful. And if you are unclear, at the end of the day you’ll be the one stuck with the problem until you find a way to communicate it clearly so that others can understand and you can all move forward.

When you’re stuck, just walk the dog

There is a particular kind of frustration that I think anyone working in AI knows well. You find a tutorial, a solution, a new framework or skill that looks like it can be implemented in the next 15 minutes. Three hours later, you’re more confused than when you started, and instead of celebrating this successful implementation, you are closer to throwing your laptop out the window.

Portia, my two-year-old German Shepherd, has been a very welcome antidote to this frustration. She’s taught me more about productivity this year than any framework or book I’ve read. There is something genuinely restorative about just getting up and going outside with a creature who is filled with nothing but love and uncomplicated joy and who asks nothing of you except your presence and perhaps a treat or a good throw of the ball.

Every time I’ve closed my laptop, gone outside, and just moved, my problem either solved itself, became less complicated, or revealed itself as not a problem worth solving. Sometimes the best thing you can do is abandon your screen and just go walk the dog. You’ll both thank you when you get home.

At the end of the day, your village is everything

In the age of AI I think sometimes people are quick to negate how valuable human connection is, but if there is anything I’ve learned in the last year it’s how important having your village is.

AI is extraordinary and can do so much, but it will never be the voice at the end of the line after a hard day and it will never give you the hug you didn’t know you needed. To my friends and family who showed up for me this year, I cannot say thank you enough.

On a very happy personal note, I got engaged at the end of last year and am getting married this summer! In a year that felt turbulent in a lot of ways, it made me more and more grateful to have found my person and be blessed to be with someone who shows up consistently and believes in me even when it’s hard to do that for myself.

Be careful what you ask for, and embrace what you get

I’ve always been someone who enjoys a challenge and wants to grow. I love learning. I love working in a field where I get to experiment with new technology everyday. I love swimming and pushing outside my comfort zone by competing in events like the Catalina Channel crossing (where you swim across the ocean in the middle of the night!). I am not afraid of being uncomfortable.

So, last year when I decided it was time for change both professionally and personally, one of the main things I was seeking were environments where I could learn and be challenged in new ways.

And the universe, with its incredible sense of humor, saidgreat, we’ve got just the year for you.

This year I remembered why we use the term “growing pains”. I don’t think life gives us more than we can handle, but I do think sometimes we ask for growth and when it actually shows up in all of its uncomfortable, inconvenient, this-is-not-how-I-planned it glory, we look at it and go wait, I didn’t mean I want to grow LIKE THIS! And that’s ok. Sometimes you need to get a little beat up and sit in the dirt for a bit. You will come out the other side and be better for it. But at the same time it’s ok to call a friend, (or walk the dog), and give yourself a breather before you get back up. But you need to keep getting up.

Can I be a real person on the internet?

This is one question I keep asking myself. I think the answer is yes, but doing so can be intimidating.

I’ve never been afraid to admit I don’t know something to my peers or teammates, but it’s a lot harder doing that online. Especially when you can open any social media platform and be immediately inundated with posts from “experts” who never seem to get confused, or hit dead ends, and are always confidently explaining the latest breakthrough that “changes everything” this week.

I think this makes it even more important to lean into the human side of the conversation. There is something uniquely human about being able to admit when we’re lost or overwhelmed and realize other people are still figuring it out too.

Working in this field is both incredibly rewarding and filled with challenges. This last year especially, I was reminded that growth is often messier and slower than I’d like it to be, and that’s ok.

So, this is a long way of saying that I’m back! I’ve missed writing and sharing what I’m learning. I have a lot more to say, and am looking forward to not only explaining what AI can do and how it does it, but also being honest about what it’s like to work in a field that moves so fast.

I’m excited to share the breakthroughs, the dead ends, the canyons between research and reality, and the moments where the best move is to step outside and hug your dog.

I’d also love to hear from you, what are some of the key lessons you’ve kept coming back to this year?
