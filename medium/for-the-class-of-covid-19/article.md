To the Class of COVID-19,

Today I, like many of you, learned that UCLA’s 2020 commencement ceremony is cancelled. The one opportunity to be united with friends, classmates, professors, and family in a celebration of learning. The chance to come together and say, “we made it!” The time to close the chapter on childhood and step fully into independence. Independence of thought, independence of finances, independence of truly being.

Graduation for many marks the beginning of the rest of your life. While we all traveled different paths to get here and have navigated both separate and intertwining roads throughout college, graduation is like a forced merge, a single location where everyone must come together before scattering into innumerable directions. It’s an opportunity for everyone to unite, acknowledge, and say thank you for everything leading up to this final ceremony. Thank you to our professors for encouraging us to think on our own. Thank you to our extracurriculars for providing us with the necessary skills and supplemental experiences to attain post-graduate employment or secure placement in further education. Thank you to our internships and jobs for providing us real, tangible experience that will translate to the workplace. Thank you to our families for providing support along the way. And most importantly thank you to our friends and peers, for showing us that we aren’t alone, for laughing with us and crying with us, for drinking with us and taking care of us, but most importantly for making us smile all along the way.

Unfortunately, due to circumstances we could not possibly predict, this closing ceremony is something the Class of COVID-19 will not get to experience. Or at least not in the same way. Sure, there may be an online alternative, and schools like UCLA remind us that “the day does not define the journey,” yet this statement feels hollow and incomplete. The college experience goes far beyond that which a commencement ceremony could ever convey. However, eliminating the ceremony and moving it online takes away much of the power, excitement, and closure associated with graduation day.

Although I am saddened by this news and not sure what the future holds for many things ranging from graduation (postponement perhaps?), if / when I’ll be able to see friends or hug my grandparents, or even have a reasonable supply of toilet paper on hand, I’d like to ask people to have hope. To remember that they have the power to make a difference in this pandemic and can choose to spread kindness, faith, and compassion instead of fear and panic.

While we may not have a clear-cut goodbye nor a formal way of coming together we can still do our part to help in this pandemic. I don’t want to just be remembered as the class that didn’t graduate, I’d much prefer to be remembered as a part of the class that did their part to help.

Cheers to hoping, to helping, and to getting through these trying times.

Sincerely,
Tula Masterman
